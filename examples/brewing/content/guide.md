# Water Chemistry for Brewing

## Overview

Water is the largest ingredient in beer by volume, yet it is often the least understood. The mineral content of your water has a profound effect on the flavor, mouthfeel, clarity, and fermentation health of your beer.

This guide covers:
- The key mineral ions and what they contribute
- How to read and interpret a water report
- Target ranges for different beer styles
- How to adjust your water using mineral additions
- When to use reverse osmosis or distilled water

---

## The Key Ions

### Calcium (Ca²⁺)

**Role:** Promotes enzyme activity, yeast health, and clarity. Reduces oxalate haze.

**Target:** 50–150 ppm for most styles. Higher (up to 200 ppm) for some English ales.

**Sources:** Gypsum (CaSO₄), Calcium Chloride (CaCl₂)

**Too low (<50 ppm):** Sluggish fermentation, haze, yeast flocculation problems.  
**Too high (>250 ppm):** Harsh minerality in the finish.

---

### Sulfate (SO₄²⁻)

**Role:** Accentuates hop bitterness and dryness. Essential for hop-forward styles.

**Target:**
- Malt-forward: 50–100 ppm
- Balanced: 100–150 ppm
- Hop-forward: 150–300 ppm
- Very hop-forward: 300–500 ppm (for some West Coast IPAs)

**Source:** Gypsum (CaSO₄)

**Character:** "Dry," crisp, enhances perceived bitterness without adding actual IBUs.

---

### Chloride (Cl⁻)

**Role:** Accentuates malt sweetness, body, and roundness.

**Target:**
- Hop-forward: 50–75 ppm
- Balanced: 75–100 ppm
- Malt-forward: 100–150 ppm

**Source:** Calcium Chloride (CaCl₂), Sodium Chloride / table salt (NaCl)

**Character:** "Soft," round, enhances malt perception.

**Important ratio:** The sulfate-to-chloride ratio (SO₄:Cl) shapes overall beer character. A ratio of 2:1 or higher favors hops; 1:2 or lower favors malt; 1:1 is balanced.

---

### Sodium (Na⁺)

**Role:** Enhances overall roundness and mouthfeel at low levels. Unpleasant at high levels.

**Target:** Below 75 ppm. Avoid exceeding 150 ppm.

**Source:** Sodium Chloride, Sodium Bicarbonate (NaHCO₃)

---

### Magnesium (Mg²⁺)

**Role:** Yeast nutrient at low levels. Contributes dry, slightly sour bitterness at higher levels.

**Target:** Below 30 ppm. Yeast need only ~10 ppm.

**Source:** Epsom salt (MgSO₄)

**Caution:** Magnesium is a laxative at high concentrations. Keep below 30 ppm.

---

### Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and Alkalinity

**Role:** Primary pH buffer. Raises mash pH.

**Target:**
- Light lagers, Pilsners: < 50 ppm (very low alkalinity)
- Pale ales: 50–100 ppm
- Amber ales: 100–150 ppm
- Dark beers (stouts, porters): 150–250 ppm

**Notes:** High bicarbonate water is hard to work with for pale beers. It can be reduced by acidification or dilution.

## Reading a Water Report

Your municipal water report will list ion concentrations in mg/L (equivalent to ppm). Key parameters to note:

| Parameter | What to Look For |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Calcium | Should be above 50 ppm for most beers |
| Magnesium | Note if above 20 ppm |
| Sodium | Note if above 50 ppm |
| Sulfate | Compare to your target |
| Chloride | Compare to your target |
| Bicarbonate or Alkalinity | Key for pH management |
| pH | Starting pH of your water |

If you don't have a water report, use a water testing service (e.g., Ward Labs) or start with Reverse Osmosis (RO) / distilled water and build from scratch.

## Making Water Adjustments

### Step 1: Know Your Starting Water

- Get a water report or start with RO/distilled water
- Note existing ion levels

### Step 2: Choose a Target Profile

- Match your target to your beer style
- Use the classic profiles or build your own

### Step 3: Calculate Additions

For a 6-gallon batch:

| Mineral | Calcium | Magnesium | Sodium | Sulfate | Chloride | Bicarbonate |
|---------|---------|-----------|--------|---------|----------|-------------|
| Gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O), 1g/gal | +62 ppm Ca | — | — | +147 ppm SO₄ | — | — |
| Calcium Chloride (CaCl₂), 1g/gal | +72 ppm Ca | — | — | — | +127 ppm Cl | — |
| Epsom Salt (MgSO₄·7H₂O), 1g/gal | — | +26 ppm Mg | — | +103 ppm SO₄ | — | — |
| Baking Soda (NaHCO₃), 1g/gal | — | — | +73 ppm Na | — | — | +189 ppm HCO₃ |
| Pickling Lime (Ca(OH)₂), 1g/gal | +144 ppm Ca | — | — | — | — | raises pH |
| Table Salt (NaCl), 1g/gal | — | — | +104 ppm Na | — | +160 ppm Cl | — |

**Rule of thumb:** Start conservative. It is much easier to add more than to remove.

### Step 4: Adjust pH

Mash pH should be 5.2–5.4 for most beer styles.

- **Too high:** Add lactic acid (1 mL/gal ≈ drops pH by ~0.1), phosphoric acid, or acidulated malt
- **Too low:** Add calcium carbonate (chalk) or baking soda

### Step 5: Verify

Use a calibrated pH meter to measure mash pH 5 minutes into the mash. Adjust as needed.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Using too much bicarbonate** — Raises mash pH too high, causes harsh, astringent flavors
2. **Ignoring your base water** — Always account for what's already in your water
3. **Oversulfating** — More than 500 ppm sulfate can become harsh and acrid
4. **Skipping pH measurement** — Calculator estimates are a starting point; always verify
5. **Using tap water for light lagers** — Most tap water has too much bicarbonate; use RO for Pilsners
