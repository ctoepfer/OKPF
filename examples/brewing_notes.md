# Brewing Notes — American IPA Fundamentals

These notes cover the key concepts and techniques for brewing a balanced American IPA at home.

## Style Overview

American IPA is characterized by pronounced hop bitterness, flavor, and aroma. The malt
character is present but subordinate to the hops. Color ranges from golden to deep amber.
IBU typically 40–70, ABV 6–7.5%.

The style originated on the West Coast but has spread widely. Modern interpretations trend
toward juicy, hazy "New England" variants, but the classic West Coast IPA remains crisp and dry.

## Hop Selection

Cascade, Centennial, and Chinook are the backbone hops of the American IPA style.

**Cascade** is the most iconic — floral, citrusy, grapefruit-forward. Alpha acids around 5–7%.
Best used for aroma additions at flameout or dry-hop. A 1 oz dry-hop addition per gallon
yields excellent results in a 5-gallon batch.

**Centennial** brings a more assertive bitterness with citrus and floral notes. Higher alpha
acids (9–11.5%) make it efficient for bittering additions at 60 minutes. Often called "super Cascade."

**Chinook** contributes pine, spice, and grapefruit. Very high alpha acids (12–14%) — primarily
used for bittering, though late additions can add pleasant piney character.

## Malt Bill

A simple grain bill lets the hops shine:

- 90–95% American 2-row pale malt (base)
- 5–10% Crystal 40L for body and a touch of sweetness
- Optional: 1–2% Carapils or flaked oats for head retention

Shoot for an OG around 1.060–1.070 for a standard American IPA.

## Water Chemistry

West Coast IPAs benefit from sulfate-heavy water. Target:
- Sulfate: 150–250 ppm (accentuates hop bitterness and dryness)
- Chloride: 50–100 ppm (adds roundness)
- Calcium: 100–150 ppm (yeast health, clarity)

If your tap water is soft, add gypsum (CaSO4) to raise sulfate. A simple additions
calculator (Brun'Water, Brewfather) takes the guesswork out.

## Yeast Selection

American IPA calls for a clean, attenuative American ale yeast:

- **WY1056 / WLP001 / US-05**: The workhorse. Clean, highly attenuative (73–80%),
  neutral flavor profile. Lets hops dominate. Ferment at 66–68°F for cleanest results.

Avoid English strains — their esters compete with American hop character.

## Fermentation

- Start at 66°F to prevent off-flavors in early fermentation.
- Raise to 70°F in the final 2–3 days to help yeast clean up diacetyl.
- Dry-hop after primary fermentation is complete — active fermentation will blow off
  volatile hop aroma compounds.

## Dry Hopping

- Add hops at or below 70°F to preserve delicate aromatic compounds.
- 3–5 days contact time is sufficient. Longer risks "grassy" or vegetal notes.
- Cold-side dry hopping (below 35°F) can further reduce biotransformation but slows
  absorption — not recommended for beginners.

## Troubleshooting

**Flat bitterness / no aroma:** Likely caused by dry-hopping during active fermentation.
CO2 scrubs volatile aromatics. Always wait for fermentation to finish.

**Harsh or astringent bitterness:** Check your water. Very low pH or over-sparging can
extract harsh tannins. Aim for mash pH 5.2–5.4.

**Haze in a West Coast IPA:** If you want clarity, cold crash to 34°F for 48+ hours.
Gelatin finings (1 tsp per 5 gallons) help significantly. Filter if you have the equipment.

## Sample Recipe — Cascade Pale IPA (5 gallons)

| Ingredient             | Amount   | Notes                    |
|------------------------|----------|--------------------------|
| American 2-row         | 10 lb    | Base malt                |
| Crystal 40L            | 0.5 lb   | Color and body           |
| Cascade (6% AA)        | 1.5 oz   | 60 min — bittering       |
| Cascade (6% AA)        | 0.5 oz   | 10 min — flavor          |
| Cascade (6% AA)        | 1 oz     | Dry-hop, 4 days          |
| US-05 yeast            | 1 packet |                          |

- OG: 1.054
- FG: 1.010
- ABV: ~5.8%
- IBU: ~35
